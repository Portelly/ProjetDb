The neuromorphic bug eye cam: A 180-degree visual system for drones

  • By John Hewitt on May 23, 2013 at 12:02 pm
  • Comment

Share This article

7
inShare

Autonomous surveillance drones are basically cameras with wings. While high-resolution cameras focused on the ground might need to be optimized for contrast sensitivity and feature extraction, those that look around the sky need a wide FOV with good resolution. A new device known as the Curvace (Curved Artificial Compound Eye) has recently been developed to meet these kinds of demands. It is not so much a camera as it a complete visual system.

The vertebrate eye, such as your own, depends on a complex control system to bring its full resolving power to bear. Synchronizing the movement of the eye, not only with head movements and body movements, but with body motion, and the movements of the eye’s binocular partner, while tracking a moving object is not just difficult — it is for all practical purposes, a digital impossibility. For these reasons, the multi-lensed eye of the invertebrate is increasingly seen as an attractive solution to the basic problem of limited field of view.

Compound plenoptic designs like the Pelican offer one solution for differentially focusing on objects at different depths. We recently reported on a hemispheric compound eye under development at the University of Illinois that has a 180-degree FOV. These artificial ommatidia are a beginning, but collecting the light is only one small part of the equation. The larger problem is to mate each channel to local circuitry that can maintain contrast resolution at the upper end of a huge dynamic range in isolation from its neighbors. At the low end of the dynamic range, color vision is nearly impossible, particularly in the face of limited bandwidth illumination. Trying to identify the color of a car under a monochromatic orange streetlight is difficult even for us.

Current cameras that only adjust for an overall average light level will wash out many important image details in direct sunlight. This problem is addressed with the Curvace by using an adaptive front end based on a design originally proposed by analog chip pioneer Carver Mead (who, incidentally, coined the phrase “Moore’s law.”) Each signal channel uses a FET circuit with feedback to give a 10-decade range of smooth response to local light level. The signal is then low-pass filtered before being multiplexed across each vertical element of the array and sent to a 10-bit ADC. The now-digital signal is serialized on a bus and sent for offline calculation of the optic flow.

Optic flow is what you are really interested in for piloting a drone. It is computed as a vector field where each pixel is not a light intensity, but a magnitude and direction of motion. It is not yet known at what point, if any, this kind of simple motion analysis is implemented in biological visual systems, but something similar is likely to be used. While the Curvace is considered “neuromorphic” by virtue of the analog VLSI (very-large-scale integration) circuits, true “spiking pixels”, as used in a few cutting edge research efforts, have not yet been incorporated into the current design. Neuroscientists are only now beginning to accept the fact that not only the timing of spikes, but their meaning changes dramatically in response to the environment, the state of the neuron that generates them, and the immediate needs of the organism that hosts the neuron.

Like the Illinois eye, the Curvace has a horizontal FOV of 180 degrees. Each column gathers light from a 60-degree FOV in the vertical plane. The device also has on-board accelerometers and gyros so that it knows both where it is in space, and how it is moving. Its design is taken directly from that of the fruit fly eye, although its overall size is more comparable to that of a crab or trilobyte. It uses 0.9 watts of power and weighs just 1.75 grams. The immediate goals of the project are to reduce the footprint even further by increasing the vertical integration of the 3D electronic circuits. Achieving the same capabilities in a package the size of fruit fly eye is a challenge for the future, but these intelligent new designs are already a tremendous advance.
